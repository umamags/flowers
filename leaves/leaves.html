Leaves of the World

Twenty of the most distinctive leaf types found across the globe

#1 Maple Leaf (Acer saccharum)

The sugar maple's iconic 5-lobed leaf turns brilliant scarlet and orange in autumn, making it the national symbol of Canada.

#2 Oak Leaf (Quercus robur)

The English oak's deeply lobed, rounded leaf is one of the most recognizable in European forests and can persist on young trees through winter.

#3 Ginkgo Leaf (Ginkgo biloba)

The fan-shaped ginkgo leaf is unique in the plant kingdom — it belongs to a living fossil lineage unchanged for over 200 million years.

#4 Fern Leaf (Pteridium aquilinum)

Bracken fern produces large, triangular, bipinnate fronds and is one of the most widespread vascular plants on Earth, colonizing open land on every continent except Antarctica.

#5 Banana Leaf (Musa)

Banana leaves can reach up to 3 metres long, making them among the largest simple leaves of any plant, and are widely used as natural serving platters and cooking wrappers.

#6 Cannabis Leaf (Cannabis sativa)

The palmate leaf of Cannabis sativa, with its serrated, finger-like leaflets arranged in opposite pairs, is one of the most instantly recognized leaves in the world.

#7 Bamboo Leaf (Phyllostachys)

Bamboo leaves are narrow, lance-shaped, and arranged in graceful alternating sprays on hollow culms — the fastest-growing woody plants on Earth.

#8 Eucalyptus Leaf (Eucalyptus)

Eucalyptus leaves hang vertically to minimise sun exposure, are rich in volatile oils that give the tree its distinctive scent, and serve as the exclusive diet of koalas.

#9 Olive Leaf (Olea europaea)

The olive's narrow, leathery, silver-grey leaf is drought-adapted with a thick cuticle and reflective underside, allowing the tree to thrive in the harsh Mediterranean climate.

#10 Pine Needle (Pinus sylvestris)

Scots pine needles grow in pairs, are blue-green, and are coated in a thick waxy cuticle that dramatically reduces water loss during cold, dry winters.

#11 Palm Leaf (Arecaceae)

Palm leaves, or fronds, are either fan-shaped (palmate) or feather-shaped (pinnate) and can reach several metres in length, providing iconic tropical canopy cover.

#12 Fig Leaf (Ficus carica)

The common fig's large, deeply lobed leaf has a rough, sandpaper-like texture from short stiff hairs, and produces a milky latex sap when broken.

#13 Lotus Leaf (Nelumbo nucifera)

The sacred lotus leaf is famous for its superhydrophobic "lotus effect" — water droplets bead up and roll off the waxy, nano-textured surface, taking dirt with them.

#14 Ivy Leaf (Hedera helix)

Common ivy leaves display striking heterophylly — juvenile leaves are distinctively lobed while adult leaves on flowering stems are entire and oval-shaped.

#15 Monstera Leaf (Monstera deliciosa)

The monstera's huge, perforated leaf develops its characteristic holes (fenestrations) as the plant matures, a trait thought to let wind and dappled light pass through in its rainforest habitat.

#16 Birch Leaf (Betula pendula)

Silver birch leaves are small, triangular, and doubly serrated with a long drip-tip, allowing the tree to be one of the first colonisers of open ground after disturbance.

#17 Cherry Leaf (Prunus avium)

The wild cherry leaf is oval with a finely serrated margin and bears two distinctive red glands at the base of the blade where the petiole meets the leaf.

#18 Willow Leaf (Salix babylonica)

The weeping willow's extremely long, narrow, lance-shaped leaves — up to 16 cm long but only 1–2 cm wide — give the tree its characteristic cascading, curtain-like silhouette.

#19 Clover Leaf (Trifolium)

Clover's trifoliate leaf, typically with a pale chevron marking on each leaflet, is the original shamrock and a nitrogen-fixing plant that enriches the soil around it.

#20 Rose Leaf (Rosa)

Rose leaves are pinnately compound with an odd number of sharply toothed leaflets, and their undersides are often slightly paler and dotted with glands that produce a characteristic fragrance.
